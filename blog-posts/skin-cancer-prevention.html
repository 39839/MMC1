Skin Cancer Prevention: Early Detection Saves Lives

MM

Dr. Marsha D. Mitchum

MD, MPH - Board Certified Dermatologist

• October 10, 2024 • 6 min read

Skin cancer is the most common cancer in the United States, but it is also one of the most preventable. Learn how to protect your skin year-round and spot warning signs before they become dangerous.

Why Skin Cancer Prevention Matters

More than five million cases of non-melanoma skin cancer are diagnosed in the U.S. each year. Ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun and tanning beds is the leading risk factor. While anyone can develop skin cancer, individuals with fair skin, light eyes, a history of sunburns, or a family history of melanoma carry a higher risk. People with darker skin tones are not immune—late detection often leads to more advanced disease.

Early detection dramatically improves outcomes. When melanoma—the deadliest form of skin cancer—is treated before it spreads, the five-year survival rate exceeds 99%. That is why routine self-exams, annual dermatologist visits, and smart sun habits are essential.

The ABCDEs of Melanoma

Use the ABCDE guide to evaluate moles and spots. If you notice any of the following characteristics, schedule a skin exam promptly:

  • A – Asymmetry: One half does not match the other half.
  • B – Border: Edges are irregular, ragged, or blurred.
  • C – Color: Pigment is uneven with shades of black, brown, tan, or even white, red, or blue.
  • D – Diameter: Larger than 6 millimeters (about the size of a pencil eraser), although melanomas can be smaller.
  • E – Evolving: Any change in size, shape, color, or sensation such as itching or bleeding.

In addition to melanoma, watch for new growths, sores that do not heal, or scaly patches that persist. Basal cell carcinoma may appear as a pearly bump, while squamous cell carcinoma often looks like a rough, red patch or wart-like lesion.

Daily Sun Protection Strategies

Sun safety is a 365-day commitment. UV rays penetrate clouds and reflect off water, sand, and snow. Incorporate these habits into your routine:

Choose the Right Sunscreen

  • Broad-spectrum coverage: Protects against both UVA (aging) and UVB (burning) rays.
  • SPF 30 or higher: Reapply every two hours, or more often if swimming or sweating.
  • Water-resistant formulas: Ideal for sports and outdoor activities.
  • Mineral options: Sunscreens containing zinc oxide or titanium dioxide are gentle on sensitive skin.

Layer on Physical Protection

  • Protective clothing: Wear long-sleeved shirts, pants, and tightly woven fabrics. Look for UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) labels.
  • Hats and sunglasses: Select wide-brimmed hats and UV-blocking eyewear to shield your face and eyes.
  • Seek shade: Plan outdoor activities before 10 a.m. or after 4 p.m., when UV rays are less intense.
  • Avoid tanning beds: Indoor tanning increases melanoma risk by up to 75% when used before age 35.

Performing Monthly Skin Self-Exams

Regular self-checks help you detect changes early. Use a full-length mirror and a hand mirror in a well-lit room. Examine your scalp, ears, neck, torso, arms, hands, legs, feet, and nail beds. Don't forget between your toes and the back of your knees. Take photos of spots that concern you so you can track changes over time.

If you notice anything new or evolving, call the dermatology team at Montgomery Medical Clinic. We offer same-week appointments for suspicious lesions and use dermatoscopy—a specialized magnifying tool—to evaluate spots in detail.

Professional Screenings and Advanced Care

Annual skin exams are recommended for everyone, and more frequent visits may be necessary if you have a history of skin cancer or numerous atypical moles. During your appointment, we document lesions with clinical photography, provide biopsy when needed, and offer personalized prevention plans.

If a biopsy confirms skin cancer, treatment options include surgical excision, Mohs micrographic surgery, cryotherapy, topical medications, or photodynamic therapy. Early treatment minimizes scarring and maximizes cure rates.

Supporting Skin Health from the Inside Out

Healthy skin begins with lifestyle choices. A diet rich in antioxidants—from berries, leafy greens, and colorful vegetables—supports cellular repair. Stay hydrated, avoid smoking, and manage stress to maintain your skin's resilience. Certain medications, including some antibiotics and acne treatments, can increase sun sensitivity; check with your provider about additional precautions.

Protecting your skin is a lifelong commitment, but it doesn't have to be overwhelming. Consistent habits, regular screenings, and trusted dermatology partners keep you safe under the sun.
